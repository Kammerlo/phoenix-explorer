# Phoenix Explorer — Design Context

## Users

**Primary**: Curious newcomers exploring Cardano for the first time — but the product is also used by stakers, developers, and analysts who arrive via search-engine deep links. Most sessions begin on the Home dashboard or land directly on a detail page (block, tx, address) from a search result.

**Context of use**:
- Desktop browsing during evening/weekend curiosity sessions ("what's happening on Cardano?")
- Mobile lookups while listening to a podcast or in a Discord/Twitter thread
- Light/dark mode both in active use; theme is user-controlled and persists

**Job to be done**:
- "Show me what's happening on the chain right now and let me feel its rhythm"
- "Explain what an epoch / a block / a stake pool is, by *showing* it instead of telling me"
- "Let me click into any address, tx, token, or pool and trust what I see"

## Brand Personality

Three words: **trustworthy, alive, knowable**.

- *Trustworthy*: data-first, never decorative for its own sake. No hype, no marketing voice.
- *Alive*: the chain is a living thing — slots tick, blocks land, epochs progress. The interface should reflect that pulse without being noisy.
- *Knowable*: the goal is to demystify Cardano. Animation, microcopy, and hierarchy should help newcomers build a mental model.

Anti-personality: cold, generic dashboard; crypto-bro neon; Bloomberg-terminal density without onboarding.

## Aesthetic Direction

**Data-poetic**. Make the chain visible. Lean into Cardano-native concepts — epochs, slots, blocks, stake, governance — as visual identity, not just data points. The block-chain visualizer on the home page is the spiritual center of the product; every other view should feel like an extension of it.

- Keep the existing MUI v7 palette and `theme.isDark` system as the foundation.
- Push *motion* and *spatial rhythm* harder than the current build does.
- Use Cardano's blue as a connective accent, but earn it — accents are rare and meaningful.
- Detail pages should feel like reading a well-typeset article about a specific entity, not a parameter dump.

## Motion Philosophy

**Subtle and professional**. Default timings 150–250ms. Easing is `ease-out` family (cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1) or similar). No bounce, no elastic, no spring overshoot for routine UI.

Where motion appears:
- Page-load stagger: header → primary metrics → table/charts. Total reveal under 600ms.
- Hover transitions on rows, cards, links: 150ms color/elevation change.
- Skeleton → real-content crossfade: 200ms opacity, no layout shift.
- Count-up on stat numbers (existing `react-countup`): keep, but cap at ~800ms.
- Pulsing live indicators (latest block, current epoch progress): keep, slow to a 2–3s breath.
- Respect `prefers-reduced-motion` everywhere — disable transforms, keep opacity.

Hero moments (allowed to be slightly more expressive, still under the "subtle" budget):
- Home dashboard reveal on first load
- Latest block landing animation in `BlockChainVisualizer`
- Epoch progress ring filling on detail page

## Design Principles

1. **The chain has a heartbeat** — every interface should reflect that the data is live, but never compete with the user's attention.
2. **Motion is feedback, not decoration** — animation explains state changes; if it doesn't, cut it.
3. **Newcomers first, power users second** — when there's a tradeoff, choose the one that helps a first-time visitor build a mental model. Power users will figure out shortcuts.
4. **Polish the shared primitives, not each page** — `Table`, `DetailHeader`, `StatCard`, `Card`, `LoadingWrapper`, `PageShell` are leverage points. Touch them once, ship across 21 views.
5. **Harden empty/error/loading states** — newcomers hit them first. They should teach, not just say "no data".
6. **Respect the user's theme** — light and dark must both feel intentional. Test every change in both.
